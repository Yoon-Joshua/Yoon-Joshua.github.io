Shadow Mapping

The idea of a shadow map is to represent the volume of space that is illuminated by a point light source. Think of a source like a spotlight or video projector, which emits light from a point into a limited range of directions. The volume that is illuminated—the set of points where you would see light on your hand if you held it there—is the union of line segments joining the light source to the closest surface point along every ray leaving that point.

Interestingly, this volume is the same as the volume that is visible to a perspective camera located at the same point as the light source: a point is illuminated by a source if and only if it is visible from the light source location. In both cases, there’s a need to evaluate visibility for points in the scene: for visibility, we needed to know whether a fragment was visible to the camera, to know whether to draw it in the image; and for shadowing, we need to know whether a fragment is visible to the light source, to know whether it’s illuminated by that source or not.

In both cases, the solution is the same: a depth map that tells the distance to the closest surface along a bunch of rays. In the visibility case, this is the z-buffer, and for the shadowing case, it is called a shadow map. In both cases, visibility is evaluated by comparing the depth of a new fragment to the depth stored in the map, and the surface is hidden from the projection point (occluded or shadowed) if its depth is greater than the depth of the closest visible surface. A difference is that the z buffer is used to keep track of the closest surface seen so far and is updated during rendering, whereas a shadow map tells the distance to the closest surface in the whole scene.

A shadowmap is calculated in a separate rendering pass ahead of time: simply rasterize the whole scene as usual, and retain the resulting depth map (there is no need to bother with calculating pixel values). Then, with the shadowmap in hand, you perform an ordinary rendering pass, and when you need to know whether a fragment is visible to the source, you project its location in the shadowmap (using the same perspective projection that was used to render the shadow map in the first place) and compare the looked-up value dmapd_{map}dmap​ with the actual distance ddd to the source. If the distances are the same, the fragment’s point is illuminated; if the d>dmapd > d_{map}d>dmap​, that implies there is a different surface closer to the source, so it is shadowed.

The phrase “if the distances are the same” should raise some red flags in your mind: since all the quantities involved are approximations with limited precision, we can’t expect them to be exactly the same. For visible points, the d≈dmapd \approx d_{map}d≈dmap​ but sometimes ddd will be a bit larger and sometimes a bit smaller. For this reason, a tolerance is required: a point is considered illuminated if d−dmap<ϵd − d_{map}<\epsilond−dmap​<ϵ. This tolerance ϵ\epsilonϵ is known as shadow bias.

When looking up in shadow maps it doesn’t make a lot of sense to interpolate between the depth values recorded in the map. This might lead to more accurate depths (requiring less shadow bias) in smooth areas, but will cause bigger problems near shadow boundaries, where the depth value changes suddenly. Therefore, texture lookups in shadow maps are done using nearest-neighbor reconstruction. To reduce aliasing, multiple samples can be used, with the 1-or-0 shadow results (rather than the depths) averaged; this is known as percentage closer filtering.

The above content is quoted from Fundamental of Computer Graphics. We need to pay attention to the following points.

  • Shadow mapping is suitable for point light source illumination.
  • The shadow mapping algorithm requires two render pass.
  • Because of numerical precision and discreteness of shadow map, we need a shadow bias.

Self Occlusion

Without shadow bias, we will get this result as the following figure shows. This problem is called self occlusion.

Why is it called self occlusion? As shown in the following figure, our eyes observe a point on the plane. Because the shadow map texture is discrete and composed of individual texels, the depth of this point is the same as the orange one when we generate the shadow map in the first render pass. When we test the depth in the second render pass, however, this point is deeper than the orange one. And then it is occluded by itself.

Shadow bias helps us reduce self occlusion but introduce detached shadow issue. Look at the shadow of Laura Crawford’s feet. It’s better to make the bias increase as the angle between the light and the surface normal increases.

We introduce a algorithm called second-depth shadow mapping in order to solve this detached shadow problem. This algorithm doesn’t need shadow bias. It uses the midpoint between first and second depths. But it requires objects to be watertight and augments the overhead, so it is less adopted.

Percentage Closer Soft Shadow

The shadow in previous chapter is called hard shadow. The difference between hard shadow and soft shadow is like this.

Area light sources cause soft shadows. Theoretically shadow mapping is not applicable to area light sources but in practical engineering applications we treat area light sources as point light sources.

First, let’s take a look at percentage closer filter ( PCF for short). Percentage closer filtering is designed for anti-aliasing at shadow’s edges originally.

Retrospecting the content of shadow mapping, we should compare the depth of object with shadow map at the second render pass. For PCF, we care about not only the corresponding single texel in the shadow map, but also texels around it. For example, the corresponding texel of our current pixel is the blue one in the shadow map. We compare the depth with the 3×33\times 33×3 texels region of the shadow map. If our depth is less than that in the shadow map, record the result as 1. Otherwise record the result as 0. Now we get the comparison result as the figure shows. So we average the results and then we get 0.6670.6670.667 representing the visibility of the current position. The size of filter is 3×33\times 33×3 in this case and it could be other sizes.

This is somewhat similar to convolutional operation. It’s notable that the objects of our convolution are neither the shadow map nor the shadows themselves, but the comparison results. Without PCF, the comparison result is either 1 or 0. 1 means visible and 0 means invisible. PCF helps us use decimals between 0 and 1 to represent the visibility so that the shadows become softer and the shadow aliasing is reduced.

By the way of formalization, convolution is described as

[w∗f](p)=∑q∈N(p)w(p,q)f(q)[w*f](p)=\sum_{q\in N(p)}{w(p,q)f(q)} [w∗f](p)=q∈N(p)∑​w(p,q)f(q)

PCF is described as

V(p)=∑q∈N(p)w(p,q)⋅χ+[DSM(q)−Dscene(p)]V(p)=\sum_{q\in N(p)}w(p,q)\cdot \chi^+[D_{SM}(q)-D_{scene}(p)] V(p)=q∈N(p)∑​w(p,q)⋅χ+[DSM​(q)−Dscene​(p)]

As metioned above, the objects of our convolution are neither the shadow map nor the shadows themselves.

V(x)≠χ+{[w∗DSM](q)−Dscene(p)}V(x)\neq \chi^+\{[w*D_{SM}](q)-D_{scene}(p)\} V(x)=χ+{[w∗DSM​](q)−Dscene​(p)}

The larger the filter size, the softer the shadows. On the contrary, the smaller the filter size, the shaper the shadows. What is the correct size? Take a loot at the picture below. We found that shadows farther away from the pen are softer and the shadow closer to the pen are sharper. Percentage closer soft shadow, PCSS for short, is aimed to achieve this effect through adjusting the size of filter adaptively.

Look at the figure below. The green one we call a blocker is an object blocking the light from the yellow area light source. The width of penumbra should be

wPenumbra=wLightdReceiver−dBlockerdBlockerw_{Penumbra}=w_{Light}\frac{d_{Receiver}-d_{Blocker}}{d_{Blocker}} wPenumbra​=wLight​dBlocker​dReceiver​−dBlocker​​

The larger the width of penumbra, the softer the shadows look. This proves that we should determine the size of filter based on the depth between blockers and the light source. In practice when we want to shade a point, first we will find the corresponding texel of the point in the shadow map. Then we scan a certain region around it to know whether there’s any blockers around it and how long the average depth between blockers and the light source is. We call this distance the average blocker depth.

How large is the region where we will scan and search blockers? There’re usually two methods. The first is to specify a fixed size artificially. The second is shown in the following figure. We assume that shadow map is on the near plane of frustum of the light source. We perform central projection on the area light source with shading point as the centre and then get the red region on the shadow map. This is the region within which we search blockers.

We will calculate the average depth of blockers in this region. The following figure is an example, in which the shading point’s depth is 7 and thus only the blue texels with values less than 7 are blockers. We calculate their average of depths which is the result we expect in this step.

So far PCSS has already been explained completely. In general, PCSS is divided into the below three steps.

  1. Blocker search

    Getting the average blocker depth in a certain region.

  2. Penumbra estimation

    Use the average blocker depth to determine filter size.

  3. Percentage closer filtering

Variance Soft Shadow Mapping

We know that PCSS’s overhead is so large. The main reason is due to the first and third steps above. It’s time-consuming to scan a region on the shadow map. Variance soft shadow mapping is designed to solve this problem through fast blocker search and filtering.

How to accelerate? The answer is approximation of course.

Let’s review the PCSS and modify its procedure.

In the first step we aim to approximate the average blocker depth quickly. In the second step there’s nothing we need to modify.

In the third step, we get the average depth of the region covered by the filter in the shadow map. If the depth of shading point is less than the average depth, the shading point is not occluded. If the depth of shading point is greater than the average depth, the shading point is occluded, and the visibility is approximated by Chebyshev’s inequality.

Distance Field Soft Shadow

Distance function gives the minimum distance (could be signed distance) to the closest location on a surface.

For instance, in a three-dimension space, the distance function of an unit sphere is F(x,y,z)=x2+y2+z2−r2F(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2+z^2-r^2F(x,y,z)=x2+y2+z2−r2 . The shortest distance from any point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) to the surface is F(x,y,z)=x2+y2+z2−r2F(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2+z^2-r^2F(x,y,z)=x2+y2+z2−r2 , which means that, for any point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) , the surface doesn’t appear within the distance of F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z) of its surroundings.

Ray marching to perform ray-SDF intersection

Given the SDF of all objects, how to test if the ray intersects with objects? Emitting the ray from the source, we calculate the distance field ddd of the source point, which means that the ray won’t meet with objects after marching a distance of ddd forward. So let the photon move forward a distance of ddd , and reach another location. The distance field of this new location is d′d'd′ , so similarly the photon can move forward a distance of d′d'd′ and reach another new location… Repeat this process until the ray intersects with any object or is judged to shoot towards infinity.

Use SDF to determine the percentage of occlusion.

This is also an approximation method.
